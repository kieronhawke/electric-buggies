# GOOGLE AND AI SEO PLAYBOOK — Electric Buggies

**Version 1.0.** The evidence base for how we rank, drawn from Google's own published guidance (read in full, May 2026) and from current cross-engine citation data. This is the standard every page and Guide is written to. Where it differs from the earlier strategy playbook, this document wins, because it is grounded in primary sources rather than third-party speculation.

---

## The one-line truth

What ranks now, on Google and inside its AI features, is genuinely useful, experience-led content on a technically sound site. There is no AI trick and no separate AI algorithm. The other AI engines (ChatGPT, Perplexity) reward a few extra things, but the foundation is the same, and most of those extras are just good practice rather than hacks.

---

## Part 1: Google's official position (primary source)

Read in full from Google Search Central: "Optimizing your website for generative AI features on Google Search" (updated 15 May 2026) and "Creating helpful, reliable, people-first content" (updated 10 December 2025).

**Google states plainly that optimising for its AI features is still SEO.** AI Overviews and AI Mode are built on the same index and the same core ranking and quality systems as normal Search. There is no separate AI index, no separate AI ranking system, and no special markup. Google explicitly frames the terms AEO and GEO as, from its perspective, still SEO.

**How the AI features actually pull content:**
- Retrieval-augmented generation (grounding): the core Search ranking systems retrieve relevant, fresh pages from the index, the model reviews them, summarises, and links out to the supporting pages.
- Query fan-out: the model fires several related sub-queries and pulls results for each. The practical implication is that content which covers a topic genuinely and completely can be surfaced for those related questions, not only the exact query. The caveat below matters: this does not mean mass-producing a page for every variation.

**The single biggest lever is non-commodity content.** Google draws a sharp line between commodity content (generic, common-knowledge pieces like a "7 tips" listicle that anyone, or an AI, could produce) and non-commodity content (unique expert or first-hand takes that go beyond the obvious). It says this, more than anything else in the guide, influences presence in AI search over time. The recurring test Google gives: would a visitor coming directly to your site find this genuinely satisfying.

**The helpful-content self-assessment, in plain terms.** Google asks site owners to judge their content honestly against questions such as: does it offer original information, research or analysis; is it comprehensive; does it give insight beyond the obvious; if it draws on other sources, does it add real value rather than rewriting them; is the title descriptive rather than clickbait; is it the sort of page you would bookmark or expect in a magazine or encyclopedia; is it well produced rather than sloppy or mass-produced. On expertise: is it clearly sourced, with author and About background; is the site a recognised authority; is it written or reviewed by someone who genuinely knows the topic; are there factual errors.

**E-E-A-T.** Experience, expertise, authoritativeness, trustworthiness. Google says trust is the most important of the four, and the others feed it. E-E-A-T is not itself a direct ranking factor, but the systems use signals that correlate with it, and they give it extra weight on Your Money or Your Life topics (health, finance, safety). For us, the road-legal and insurance content is the closest to YMYL, so accuracy and clear sourcing matter most there.

**Who, How, Why.** Make it clear who created the content (bylines linking to author background where a reader would expect them). Explain how it was made where relevant (for reviews, how many units tested, the results, with photographic evidence; disclose AI use where a reader would wonder). And the why should be to help people, not to rank. Producing content mainly for rankings, or mass AI-generating it to manipulate rankings, breaches Google's spam policy.

**Technical foundations (still required).** A page must be indexed and eligible to show with a snippet, and must be crawlable. Semantic HTML helps but perfect code is not required. Follow JavaScript SEO practices if using a framework. Provide a good page experience. Reduce duplicate content. Verify the site in Search Console.

**Local and ecommerce.** Google Business Profile and structured product data (including Merchant Center feeds) help products and local information appear in AI responses as well as in Search.

## Part 2: What Google explicitly says to ignore

Google names these as unnecessary for its surfaces. We do not spend effort on them:
- **llms.txt and other special AI files or markup.** Not needed to appear in AI search.
- **Chunking content into tiny pieces for AI.** Not needed. There is no ideal page length.
- **Rewriting content specifically for AI, or chasing every long-tail variation.** Not needed; the systems understand synonyms and meaning.
- **Seeking inauthentic mentions across the web.** Not helpful, and spam systems act on it.
- **Over-focusing on structured data for AI.** Not required, and there is no special schema for AI. Keep structured data for rich results, not as an AI tactic.
- **Writing to a target word count.** Google has no preferred length.
- **Faking freshness** by changing dates or churning content in and out to look fresh. It does not help. Genuinely updating content does.

This list directly contradicts several popular GEO tactics. We drop them for Google.

## Part 3: The cross-engine reality (practitioner data, treat as directional)

Google's guidance covers Google. The other AI engines use different retrieval and cite different sources. Multiple 2026 studies agree on the qualitative picture, though exact figures vary and the citation graph is volatile, so treat the numbers as directional, not precise.

- **Low overlap.** One analysis of 680 million citations (Averi, echoed by an independent Whitehat study) found only about 11 percent of domains are cited by both ChatGPT and Perplexity. Even Google's own AI Overviews and AI Mode share only around 14 percent of their cited URLs.
- **Google AI Overviews and AI Mode** draw from the same index as Search, so SEO fundamentals drive citation. If you rank, you are most of the way there. This matches Google's own statement.
- **Perplexity** retrieves in real time with no knowledge cutoff and can cite new content within hours. It has a strong recency bias (one analysis found content under 30 days old cited at around 82 percent, and a visible "2026" in titles lifting citation rates by roughly 30 percent), and it favours specific statistics, visible methodology, named sources and clear structure. It offers the most citation slots per answer and the fastest feedback loop.
- **ChatGPT** leans heavily on Bing's index and on Wikipedia, with a multi-week lag between publishing and citation. Many of its most-cited pages are off-limits (Wikipedia, government, education, major news). The levers that help are Bing presence and authentic mentions on authoritative sites, more than on-page tweaks.
- **Claude and others** are less documented but follow similar principles: authoritative, well-structured, genuinely useful sources.
- **AI traffic is small but converts far better.** Across several vendor benchmarks, AI referrals are roughly 1 percent of traffic but convert several times higher than classic Google clicks. So AI visibility is a low-volume, high-value play, not a traffic firehose, which suits a high-value product like ours.

## Part 4: The unified strategy (what we actually do)

One playbook that serves Google first and the other engines as a bonus, with no manipulative tactics. Everything here is also simply good practice.

**Do:**
1. **Non-commodity, experience-led content.** The single biggest lever. Write what only a real British premium dealer can: genuine specifics on configuration and branded fleets, the real road-legal position, total cost of ownership, sector use in practice, with real photography and real figures. Avoid generic pieces an AI could produce.
2. **Clear authorship and trust.** Real author and About information, transparent delivery, warranty, finance and returns, genuine reviews when we have them, and accurate sourcing, which matters most on the road-legal and insurance content.
3. **Excellent technical foundation.** Indexable, crawlable, fast, mobile, low duplication, verified in Search Console. This includes resolving the resorts and hotels duplication and fixing the model-page titles.
4. **Genuine helpfulness and clear structure.** Descriptive titles, sensible headings, readable paragraphs. We do this because it helps readers and, as a bonus, makes content easy to extract, not as an AI hack.
5. **Keep cornerstone content genuinely current.** Real updates with honest dates. This helps Perplexity's recency bias and is good practice. Never date-faking.
6. **Local and product data.** Google Business Profile if we have a showroom, and structured product data, for product and local visibility in AI and Search.
7. **Be present on Bing, not just Google.** Submit to Bing Webmaster Tools. ChatGPT leans on Bing, so this is the main lever for ChatGPT visibility.
8. **Earn authentic mentions and links** on relevant, authoritative sites (trade and sector press, genuine directories). This helps Google authority and the other engines alike. Never fake or buy them.
9. **Structured data for rich results** (Product, LocalBusiness, Organization, BreadcrumbList, Article), for rich results and entity clarity, not as an AI requirement.

**Do not:**
- No llms.txt or AI-only files, no content chunking, no writing to a word count.
- No AI-only rewriting and no spinning up a page for every keyword variation (that is scaled-content spam).
- No inauthentic mentions, bought links, or faked freshness.
- Do not over-invest in FAQ schema purely to win AI. Use FAQ because buyers genuinely ask the questions.

## Part 5: How this updates our earlier plan

The earlier strategy playbook's GEO section leaned on third-party advice (front-load the answer in the first 200 words as a rule, maximise data density, structure for chunking, chase multi-channel mentions). Google's primary guidance shows some of that is unnecessary for its surfaces. The net changes:
- **Stop treating GEO as a separate discipline for Google.** It is SEO. One playbook.
- **Keep answering clearly and early, and using specifics and real numbers,** but as good writing and because it helps Perplexity and ChatGPT, not as a Google AI hack.
- **Keep FAQ content for users; stop treating FAQ schema as an AI-citation lever.**
- **Add two genuine cross-engine levers we had underweighted:** Bing Webmaster Tools, and authentic mentions and digital PR.
- **Reaffirm and elevate the priority:** non-commodity, experience-led content. This raises the bar on every page and Guide we write.

## Part 6: What this means for the content I write

Every page and Guide will be written to Google's helpful-content standard: original, experience-led, specific, accurate, clearly authored, well structured and genuinely useful. That is exactly what Google rewards and exactly what the premium brand needs. The generic placeholder content on the live site is, in Google's own terms, commodity content, which is the single clearest thing to fix. Replacing it with non-commodity, expert content is therefore the highest-value work, and it is where we start.
